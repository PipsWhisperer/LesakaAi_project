\section{Background}

Botswana's agricultural sector plays a crucial role in the country's economy, with livestock farming being a significant contributor to rural livelihoods and food security. According to the Botswana Ministry of Agricultural Development and Food Security, the cattle population in Botswana exceeds 2 million head, making it one of the largest cattle populations per capita in Africa \cite{botswana_agriculture_2023}.

However, traditional livestock management methods in Botswana face several challenges. Farmers rely heavily on manual observation and periodic physical inspections to monitor cattle health, which is labor-intensive and often results in delayed detection of health issues. The lack of real-time monitoring systems means that diseases and health problems are often identified only when symptoms become severe, leading to increased mortality rates and economic losses.

The emergence of Internet of Things (IoT) technologies offers significant potential for transforming agricultural practices in developing regions. IoT-enabled sensor networks can provide continuous, real-time monitoring of livestock health parameters, enabling early detection of health issues and timely intervention. However, the implementation of IoT systems in agricultural contexts presents unique challenges related to data governance, privacy protection, and system reliability in resource-constrained environments.

\section{Problem Statement}

The current livestock management practices in Botswana suffer from several critical limitations:

1. **Delayed Health Detection**: Without real-time monitoring, health issues are often detected only after symptoms become severe, reducing treatment effectiveness and increasing mortality rates.

2. **Limited Data Governance**: Existing agricultural systems lack robust data governance frameworks, leading to inconsistent data quality, security vulnerabilities, and privacy concerns for farmers.

3. **Poor Data Integrity**: Many agricultural IoT systems do not implement proper database normalization, resulting in data redundancy, update anomalies, and maintenance challenges.

4. **Privacy Concerns**: Farmer data, including personal information and location data, is often stored without adequate privacy protections, raising concerns about data misuse and unauthorized access.

5. **Scalability Issues**: Traditional systems cannot scale to handle the volume of telemetry data generated by large-scale IoT deployments, limiting their applicability for commercial operations.

These challenges highlight the need for a comprehensive data governance framework that addresses database design, data integrity, privacy protection, and system scalability specifically tailored for agricultural IoT contexts in developing regions like Botswana.

\section{Motivation}

The motivation for this project stems from both practical and academic considerations:

**Practical Motivation:**
- Economic losses due to livestock diseases in Botswana are estimated at millions of Pula annually \cite{botswana_livestock_2022}
- Improved livestock health monitoring could significantly reduce these losses and improve farmer livelihoods
- Technology adoption in Botswana's agricultural sector is increasing, creating opportunities for IoT solutions
- Privacy concerns are becoming increasingly important as digital systems become more prevalent in agriculture

**Academic Motivation:**
- Limited research exists on data governance frameworks specifically designed for agricultural IoT in developing regions
- The intersection of database normalization, privacy-by-design, and agricultural IoT presents an interesting research area
- The project provides an opportunity to apply theoretical database concepts (3NF normalization) in a practical context
- The project aligns with INFS 401 module requirements for data governance and database design

\section{Aim}

The aim of this project is to design and implement a comprehensive data governance framework for agricultural IoT systems in Botswana, with a focus on database design, data integrity, privacy protection, and system scalability.

\section{Objectives}

The specific objectives of this project are:

1. Design and implement a Third Normal Form (3NF) database schema for agricultural telemetry data, ensuring data integrity and eliminating redundancy.

2. Develop a data validation engine that enforces data quality standards and prevents invalid data entry.

3. Implement a Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) system to protect sensitive farmer data and ensure privacy-by-design.

4. Create a data quality scoring system to monitor and assess the quality of telemetry data over time.

5. Validate the system through comprehensive testing to ensure compliance with data governance standards.

6. Document the system according to academic standards, including normalization proofs, security design, and performance analysis.

\section{Risks}

\subsection{Technical Risks}
- **Database Performance**: SQLite may not scale to handle very large datasets, though this is acceptable for the expected data volume in this project
- **Data Validation Complexity**: Implementing comprehensive validation rules may be more complex than initially anticipated
- **RBAC Implementation**: Designing an appropriate permission matrix requires careful consideration of user roles and access patterns

\subsection{Project Risks}
- **Time Constraints**: The academic semester timeline may limit the depth of implementation for some features
- **Learning Curve**: Some technologies (Pydantic, advanced SQLite features) may require additional learning time
- **Testing Limitations**: Limited access to real-world agricultural environments may constrain comprehensive testing

\subsection{Academic Risks}
- **Scope Creep**: The temptation to add features beyond the INFS 401 scope (e.g., multi-agent systems) must be carefully managed
- **Documentation Quality**: Balancing technical depth with academic writing standards requires careful attention
- **Assessment Alignment**: Ensuring the project clearly demonstrates INFS 401 learning outcomes without overlapping with COMP 401
